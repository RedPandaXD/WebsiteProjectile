Semi-Automatic

The Semi-Automatic type usually could hold slightly more rubber bands (possibly 8-15). These are accurate to an extent, probably hitting the target 7 out 10 times withing 10 feet, and have faster reload. Usually these only require the pull of the trigger, instead of having the user to manually reload after every shot.

Challenges

The hardest obstacle to overcome when building semi-automatic projectiles is the fact that it has to repeatedly shoot rubber bands smoothly per pull on the trigger, and have a somewhat accurate shot and consistent range. Snipers will provide a very accurate shot, but the accuracy all relies upon how simple the projectile is built. With semi-automatic projectiles, the mechanism needs to provide the next rubber band quickly while having the simplicity required for accuracy. I find these very difficult to design, but the solution is either to program a controller for it, which is very simple, otherwise build completely out of mechanisms. Using a trigger that powers a motor continuously would not be best, as it would drain the power of the battery quickly and over time easily wear out the motor from a constant on and off tension in spinnning. I find Lego EV3 and Technic materials to be most ideal, as semi-automatic projectiles sometimes have to withstand massive force on a single area and require a strong, durable motor. Lego EV3 is a programmable set of parts with a mini computing device, strong and medium motors, and a touch sensor along with other sensors. Lego Technic provides long, sturdy beams and connectors to give access to design a very durable structure for the projectile, making it very reliable so the devices will never fall out and break. For completely mechanical projectiles, the maximum rubber bands that can be loaded at once is around 5 rubber bands, due to the control of pressure so the parts do not break. Mechanical ones get easier to pull the trigger as the amount loaded gets fewer, which makes it slightly more inconsistant and harder to use.
